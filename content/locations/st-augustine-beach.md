## The Coastal Factor: What Salt Air Does to Your Property

Living within a mile of the Atlantic Ocean means your property is constantly bathed in salt-laden air. This isn't just a cosmetic issue — it's an active deterioration process:

### Salt Deposits on Siding
Airborne salt particles settle on siding, windows, and trim. Salt is hygroscopic — it pulls moisture from the air and holds it against surfaces. This creates a persistently damp microenvironment where mold and mildew thrive, even on walls that receive direct sunlight.

### Accelerated Roof Staining
Roofs on Anastasia Island develop black algae streaks and green growth faster than inland properties. The combination of salt moisture retention and ocean humidity creates ideal conditions for Gloeocapsa magma, the algae responsible for those dark roof stains.

### Concrete and Paver Damage
Salt crystallization inside porous concrete and paver surfaces causes spalling — surface flaking and pitting that worsens over time. Regular cleaning removes salt deposits before they cause permanent damage.

### Metal Corrosion
Gutters, downspouts, flashing, railings, and decorative hardware corrode significantly faster near the coast. While pressure washing can't prevent all corrosion, removing salt buildup slows the process.

### Paint and Coating Failure
Salt and UV exposure break down paint and protective coatings faster at the beach. Homes that might go 10 years between painting inland often need repainting every 5–7 years on the island. Regular cleaning removes the salt and biological growth that accelerates coating failure.
